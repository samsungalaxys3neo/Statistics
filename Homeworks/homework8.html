Analogies between Stochastic Processes and Combinatorics

> Preamble: Comparison between HMWK 7 (Random Walks) and HMWK 4 (Law of Large Numbers)

Homework 7 simulated a 1D Random Walk. Every week ($n$ total steps), a server experienced an event $X_i$ with two possible outcomes: $+1$ (secure, probability $1-p$) or $-1$ (breached, probability $p$). The final score was the sum $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$.

A "pure" simulation of the Law of Large Numbers (LLN) would observe a simpler Bernoulli process, $Y_i$, with outcomes $\{1, 0\}$ (e.g., success/failure). The LLN states that the sample mean $\bar{Y}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum Y_i$ converges to the success probability $p$.

> Fundamental Similarities and Differences

  • Similarity (The Bernoulli Process): Both models are founded on i.i.d. Bernoulli trials (independent and identically distributed). The previous homework was a simple linear transformation of a standard Bernoulli process ($K_n$, the *number* of successes): $$ S_n = (+1) \cdot K_n + (-1) \cdot (n - K_n) = 2K_n - n $$ Simulating $S_n$ is equivalent to simulating $K_n$. The convergence of the empirical distribution of $S_n$ to its theoretical form (which we saw in HMWK 7) is a direct manifestation of the LLN.
  • Difference (The State Space): HMWK 7 focuses on the cumulative "score" (a state space $S_n \in \{-n, -n+2, ..., n\}$), typical of Random Walks. A standard LLN simulation focuses on the "count" ($K_n \in \{0, 1, ..., n\}$). The concepts are mathematically equivalent, but the physical interpretation (a "walk" vs. a "success rate") differs.

> Mathematical Properties and Combinatorial Relations

This is where the true depth emerges. The Bernoulli process is the bridge connecting probability to combinatorics.

▶ Binomial Coefficients and Pascal's Triangle

Why is the final distribution called "Binomial"? Because it's built with binomial coefficients. A binomial coefficient, $\binom{n}{k}$, counts the number of ways to choose $k$ items from a set of $n$, without replacement and without regard to order.

In our HMWK 7, to get a final score $S_n$ (which is equivalent to having $k = (S_n+n)/2$ successes), there are exactly $\binom{n}{k}$ unique paths (trajectories) that lead to that result. If $p=0.5$, every path is equally likely, so the probability of a final score is $\binom{n}{k} / 2^n$.

Pascal's Triangle is the geometric visualization of these coefficients. Each number in the triangle is the sum of the two numbers above it. This corresponds exactly to the number of paths to reach a point in a Random Walk: to arrive at step $n$, position $k$, you must have come from either $(n-1, k-1)$ or $(n-1, k)$.

Fundamental Relation (Pascal's Rule): $$ \binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k} $$ (Num. paths for $k$ successes in $n$ steps) = (Num. paths for $k-1$ successes in $n-1$ steps, followed by a success) + (Num. paths for $k$ successes in $n-1$ steps, followed by a failure)

▶ Binomial Expansion

The Binomial Theorem describes the algebraic expansion of a power of a binomial:

$$ (x+y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k} x^k y^{n-k} $$

The analogy is perfect. If we set $x = (1-p)$ (probability of $+1$) and $y = p$ (probability of $-1$), the expansion represents the *sum* of the probabilities of *all possible outcomes* after $n$ steps.

  • The term $\binom{n}{k} (1-p)^k p^{n-k}$ is exactly the probability of having $k$ successes and $n-k$ failures (the Binomial PMF).
  • The entire sum is $ \sum P(K_n=k) = ((1-p) + p)^n = 1^n = 1 $. This proves that the sum of all probabilities in our model is 1.
  • If $x=1, y=1$, we get $(1+1)^n = 2^n = \sum \binom{n}{k}$, which counts the total number of all possible unique paths in $n$ steps (if $p=0.5$).

▶ The Fibonacci Sequence (A Different Connection)

The connection with the Fibonacci Sequence ($F_n = F_{n-1} + F_{n-2}$) is less direct, but it exists via Pascal's Triangle. If you sum the numbers along the "shallow diagonals" of Pascal's Triangle, you get the Fibonacci sequence.

This is because $F_n$ can be expressed in terms of binomial coefficients:

$$ F_{n} = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{n-k-1}{k} $$

Combinatorial Interpretation: While $\binom{n}{k}$ counts paths in a $\{+1, -1\}$ Random Walk, $F_n$ counts the number of ways to "climb $n$ stairs" taking steps of either 1 or 2 stairs. It's a different kind of stochastic/combinatorial process, where the current state depends on the *two* previous states (not just one), and the choice at each step isn't a simple Bernoulli trial (it's a choice between "step 1" or "step 2").

So, while not analogous to HMWK 7, $F_n$ belongs to the same family of "combinatorial counting" problems and shares a mathematical root (Pascal's Triangle) with binomial coefficients.

> Conclusion

Homework 7 was a physical simulation (a "Random Walk") of a Bernoulli process. Homework 8 (this analysis) reveals that the structure of that process is governed entirely by the laws of combinatorics.

The binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{k}$ is the unifying concept:

  1. It counts the number of paths (Combinatorics, Pascal's Triangle).
  2. It defines the probability distribution (Statistics, Binomial Distribution).
  3. It is the coefficient in the algebraic expansion (Algebra, Binomial Theorem).
The simulation in HMWK 4 empirically demonstrated (via the LLN) the validity of these theoretical connections.
